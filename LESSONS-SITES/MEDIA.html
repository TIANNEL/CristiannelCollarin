Electronic Media

Definitions

  • Electronic Media: Media that allows users to access content through electronics or electromechanical energy, such as video recordings, audio, and internet material[cite: 402, 404].
  • Static Media: Primarily print media that, while often generated electronically, does not require electronics for the end-user to access the printed form[cite: 403].
  • Media Roles: Defined by their societal functions: to educate, inform, and entertain through news, documentaries, and public service announcements [cite: 350-353].

History and Theoretical Impact

  • Technological Shift: The advancement of hardware and software has moved society from traditional broadcasting to a nonlinear, digital environment where content is easily shared and saved[cite: 335, 398, 400].
  • Historical Milestones:
    • Morse Code: Developed by Samuel F.B. Morse for coded messaging via dots and dashes[cite: 223, 224].
    • Heinrich Hertz: Proved that signals could be transmitted wirelessly[cite: 226].
    • Guglielmo Marconi: Invented a device for wireless communication, leading to the first radio news bulletins by 1916[cite: 228].

Media Landscape and Regulation

  • Philippine Regulation: Government bodies like the MTRCB, Optical Media Board, and the National Council for Children's Television manage content control and classification[cite: 321, 54].
  • R.A. 10175 (Cybercrime Prevention Act of 2012): A major effort to address digital threats while balancing concerns regarding free expression[cite: 307, 308].
  • Prohibited Acts: Under Philippine law, acts like illegal access, data interference, cybersex, child pornography, and online libel are strictly regulated [cite: 310-317].

Legal, Ethical, and Societal Issues

  • Intellectual Property: Key issues include Copyright Infringement (violating exclusive creative rights) and Digital Piracy (unlawful duplication of media)[cite: 237, 240, 288].
  • Academic Integrity: Plagiarism involves adopting another's language or concepts without acknowledgment[cite: 248, 249].
  • Online Safety:
    • Cyberbullying: Using electronic technology to humiliate or harass others[cite: 260].
    • Phishing: Deceiving individuals into sharing sensitive data by impersonating a trustworthy entity[cite: 274].
  • Digital Divide: The gap in access to modern ICT between different individuals or communities[cite: 305].
